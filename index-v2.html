The conditions that produce
forced labor. v.2026

Forced labor is a hidden phenomenon. The people most exploited are the least able to report. Authoritarian states control what gets recorded; informal economies leave no paper trail; survivors who escape often cannot approach authorities. Conventional prevalence indices systematically under-count exactly where structural risk is highest — because the same conditions that produce forced labor also suppress the data those indices depend on.

FLSRI measures the structural conditions directly — institutional, economic, demographic, financial — and treats the gap between high structural risk and low reported prevalence as a feature, not a discrepancy.

What you are looking at

The map is structural risk, not victim count.

A country shaded dark on this map has the institutional, economic, and demographic conditions under which forced labor flourishes — not necessarily the highest documented number of victims. The two often diverge. Walk Free's Global Slavery Index puts North Korea at the world's highest prevalence; FLSRI cannot, because the data those measurements depend on is suppressed by the same state apparatus that produces the conditions. Where structural risk is high and reported prevalence is low, that gap is the finding.

Two stories

The index tells two different risk stories.

Conventional prevalence indices compress these into a single rank. FLSRI separates them by phase. The countries that produce exploitable populations are not necessarily the same countries that retain the proceeds.

Fragile-origin risk

Where populations become exploitable.

Conflict, displacement, climate disasters, weak institutions, household economic precarity, and absent social protection — the conditions that produce populations available for predatory recruitment. These countries score high on Phase 1 because the structural pre-conditions for forced labor are concentrated there.

Why this matters

Conventional indices conflate the two.

Treating a global league table of "where is forced labor worst" as a single ranking erases the difference between countries that produce exploitable populations (typically conflict-affected, displacement-pressured, institutionally fragile) and countries that retain the proceeds (typically high-income, well-governed, with sophisticated financial systems). Policy responses diverge accordingly: the former call for humanitarian and developmental interventions; the latter call for AML reform, supply-chain due diligence, and beneficial-ownership transparency. FLSRI's phase decomposition makes that distinction explicit.

Phase anatomy

Three phases. One system.

The phases are sequential: people are recruited from somewhere, exploited under some institutional conditions, and the proceeds flow through some financial channel. A country has high structural risk when all three align — and that's why the composite is a geometric mean rather than an arithmetic one.

Phase 1

Recruitment

Where do exploitable populations come from?

The structural conditions that produce populations vulnerable to recruitment into exploitative labor. Captures both acute disruptions (disasters, conflict, displacement) and chronic conditions (household economic precarity, absent institutional protection). High Phase 1 scores indicate large recruitment-vulnerable populations.

Distribution patterns

The phase plane reveals structure.

Plotting recruitment risk against exploitation risk reveals four distinct profile clusters. The composite score compresses these into a single rank; the phase plane preserves them.

Quadrant 1

Full-system risk

High on every phase. Recruitment vulnerability, weak guardianship, and monetization channels all align. The composite top tier.

Quadrant 2

Fragile-origin profile

High recruitment and exploitation, lower monetization. Conditions produce vulnerable populations and exploitation persists, but proceeds-retention infrastructure is weaker.

Quadrant 3

Financial-destination profile

Low recruitment and exploitation, high monetization. Forced labor does not occur at structural scale here; the monetization phase identifies these as financial-system environments where illicit value can be moved or retained.

Quadrant 4

Low structural risk

Low across all phases. The conditions for forced labor to flourish at structural scale are not present.

Reading the quadrants

One number compresses four very different stories.

A composite score of 0.30 can mean a fragile-origin country with active conflict and weak institutions, or a high-income financial center with elevated AML risk. The composite ranks them similarly; the structural reality is unrelated. Quadrant placement is a more honest answer to "what is happening here?" than the rank.

Why borders matter

Forced labor flows through corridors, not states.

The Sahel-to-Maghreb migration corridor, the Andean-Amazon trafficking routes, the South Asian labor-migration system, the Mekong fisheries: each is a regional structural-risk cluster that no single-country index can capture. The HH clusters above are not coincidences — they reflect the geography of conflict spillover, shared institutional weakness, and migration routes where vulnerable populations cross borders into jurisdictions whose enforcement gaps are effectively continuous with their origin country.

The HL "isolated risk" tag and LH "regional protective" tag matter for policy. A country flagged HL is a structural-risk concentration its neighbors don't share — typically driven by domestic conditions (state policy, single-sector exposure) rather than regional spillover. An LH country is comparatively well-positioned, but the surrounding pressure means policy cannot be purely domestic.

Methodology

Every choice is traceable.

The index is built on a discipline of theoretical anchoring, transparent aggregation, and honest propagation of missing data. Every methodological decision traces to either a source codebook or construct theory.

Anchor A

Theoretical maximum, not observed maximum.

Every indicator is scaled to [0, 1] where 1.0 represents the theoretical worst on that dimension — not the worst-observed country. For percentages, the anchor is 100% by definition. For ordinal indices (V-Dem, GITOC, Basel), the anchor is the source codebook maximum. For per-capita disaster mortality, the anchor is the population fraction (entire population affected per year). For labor productivity (which has no codebook ceiling), the World Bank international subsistence wage anchors the high-risk floor and the OECD G7 mean anchors the low-risk frontier.

The discipline matters because the index uses multiplicative aggregation across phases. Multiplication only has consistent meaning when factors are anchored consistently. And it means the scale is stable across data updates: adding a country to the sample does not reshuffle existing scores.

Aggregation

Indicator → domain → phase → composite.

Each level applies a different rule, deliberately:

Indicator → Domain D = mean of available indicators (NaN-tolerant)
Domain → Phase P = exp(Σ ln(D) / n_observed) — geometric mean
Reinforcement R_adj = R × (0.70 + 0.60 × E × M)
Composite FLSRI = ∛(max(0.05, R_adj) × max(0.05, E) × max(0.05, M))

The geometric mean at the phase and composite levels enforces the routine-activity-theory thesis: forced labor at structural scale requires recruitment vulnerability, exploitation conditions, and monetization architecture simultaneously. A country strong on one dimension but absent on another cannot reach the top tier — and that is the correct compositional logic for the construct.

Imputation

Missing data is not fabricated.

Indicator values that are missing in the source data propagate as NaN through the aggregation hierarchy. A country with sparse coverage produces NaN at affected aggregation levels rather than an imputed value. This is more honest than fabricating, and it is consistent with the structural-versus-prevalence thesis: countries where data is suppressed by political conditions are themselves the structural-risk cases the index exists to surface.

One narrowly justified exception applies to civil documentation (1.4.2): high-income OECD countries are zero-filled because universal birth registration is the institutional baseline in those jurisdictions, and absence in source data reflects non-reporting of saturated coverage rather than a coverage gap.

Theoretical grounding

Routine activity theory, three phases.

The framework operationalizes Routine Activity Theory (Cohen and Felson, 1979). Crime persists where three conditions converge: vulnerable populations, weak guardianship, and motivated offenders with means. Each becomes a phase. Each phase decomposes into domains capturing distinct mechanisms. Each domain decomposes into indicators with documented sources, transforms, and theoretical anchors.

Validation thesis

Divergence from prevalence is informative.

FLSRI scores are expected to show moderate but imperfect correlation with prevalence-based forced-labor indices (Walk Free GSI, ILO Global Estimates, US Trafficking in Persons reports). Where FLSRI flags high structural risk and prevalence indices report low cases, the gap itself is meaningful — it suggests forced labor that is institutionally invisible. The validation strategy does not treat prevalence indices as ground truth; it treats their divergence from FLSRI as informative about the boundary of what each measurement captures.

Limits

What this index cannot tell you.

Every measurement has boundaries. These are the ones we know about and document.

Closed-state under-sensitivity

The structural-versus-prevalence thesis applies to FLSRI itself.

Closed states (DPRK, Eritrea, Turkmenistan) evade the data sources the index relies on. Where data exists, scores are extreme; where data is missing, the geometric-mean phase aggregation produces NaN or artificially low scores. This is the construct working correctly while exposing the boundary of what any indicator can measure.

Per-capita amplification

Small populations can over-rank on per-capita measures.

Small countries with concentrated disaster shocks or asylum inflows can produce per-capita scores that overstate structural risk relative to larger countries with absolutely larger but proportionally smaller flows. Slovenia's 2023 floods, Costa Rica's Nicaraguan asylum inflow, and Vanuatu's cyclones are examples in the data.

Reinforcement calibration

The reinforcement multiplier is provisional.

The current coefficients (0.70 / 0.60) imply a multiplier range of [0.70, 1.30] but realize a smaller range in observed data because no country reaches the joint-maximum exploitation × monetization product the formula assumes. Subject to lock-time recalibration once all phases stabilize.

Validation thesis

Moderate correlation with prevalence is the expected state.

FLSRI is not validated against prevalence indices as ground truth. The expected pattern is moderate correlation with significant divergence — strong enough to confirm both measure related phenomena, weak enough to confirm FLSRI captures information prevalence misses. The divergences are the point.
