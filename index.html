The Forced Labor Structural Risk Index

Understanding the Structural Conditions that Enable Forced Labor.

Trafficking systems and forced labor networks operate as economic systems. They recruit where populations are exposed, exploit where institutions fail to intervene, and monetize where financial systems obscure illicit proceeds. Forced labor persists under identifiable structural conditions, including high vulnerability, weak institutional protection, and incentives for profitable exploitation. This index measures those conditions across 195 countries.

Framework

The theoretical architecture behind the Forced Labor Structural Risk Index.

An Economic System

Forced labor is not isolated criminal violence. It is an economic system operated by trafficking networks and transnational criminal organizations. These enterprises recruit workers, extract labor under coercion, and convert the proceeds into financial assets—generating an estimated $236 billion in illegal profits annually.

At that scale, this is one of the most profitable illicit economies in the world. It persists not because enforcement is absent, but because structural conditions across entire countries create the environment in which these operations are rational, low-risk, and self-sustaining.

Theoretical Foundation

The FLSRI is grounded in Routine Activity Theory (Cohen & Felson, 1979), which holds that crime occurs when three elements converge in time and space: a motivated offender, a suitable target, and the absence of a capable guardian. Applied to trafficking systems:

Suitable Targets
Populations made vulnerable by displacement, economic precarity, or institutional exclusion
Measured through Phase 1: structural conditions that generate exposed, recruitable populations
Absence of Guardians
Weak enforcement, geographic concealment, and institutional gaps that fail to detect or intervene
Measured through Phase 2: conditions that enable sustained coercion without institutional interruption
Motivated Offenders
Trafficking networks that operate as rational economic enterprises, sustained by financial infrastructure that conceals proceeds
Measured through Phase 3: conditions that enable value extraction and reinvestment
A structural reframe
Trafficking persists where all three conditions co-exist. Enforcement alone cannot alter the structural conditions while the enabling environment persists.
The FLSRI measures the environment, not the actors

A self-reinforcing loop

Trafficking is not linear. It is a cycle operated by criminal enterprises that perpetuates itself. Proceeds from monetization corrupt institutions and regenerate the structural conditions that supply the next cycle of victims.

Three phases

The FLSRI measures structural risk across three interconnected phases of the trafficking lifecycle. Each phase maps to a component of routine activity theory.

Phase 1: Recruitment

Where traffickers gain access to victims

Structural conditions that generate exposed, immobile, or coercible populations.
